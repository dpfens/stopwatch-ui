Why This Exists

A few years back, I went to a track meet to watch my old college team compete. I tried timing some runners with my phone's stopwatch, wanting to see how the team had progressed since I was on it.

It was a disaster. I couldn't remember which split belonged to which runner, couldn't record anything, and ended up with a confusing mess of numbers.

That seemed like a pretty ridiculous problem to have in 2020, but buying a $500 timing system wasn't happening. A couple months later COVID lockdown started, so I built my own.

I let Reddit know about it, and turns out hundreds of people had the same problem. This is version 2. Same idea, but rebuilt to be more reliable, easier to use, and easier for me to work on.

What's Different in Version 2

upgrade Actually Upgradeable

The original version worked, but making changes was a nightmare. Everything was tightly coupled. The way data was stored and how timing worked heavily limited what features were possible. Adding new functionality meant potentially breaking everyone's existing data.

This version uses a completely different approach. Instead of storing calculated durations, it stores a sequence of events (start, stop, split, lap). Your timing data becomes a timeline of what actually happened, not a rigid snapshot.

info
Result: I can add features, fix bugs, and improve things without your data becoming incompatible.
